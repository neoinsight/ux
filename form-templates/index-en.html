Providing Help with Form Questions

You should strive for clear simple questions, but sometimes a little help is needed. We tried these Level 2 and Level 3 methods in many studies and they worked well.

Use an overlay to tell them where to find the answer

Describe how they can find the answer

Use a 60% width overlay with a Close button for help on a form. In our research, we had people tell us which version of browser they were using and this overlay told them how to find the version number in each browser. Even on a phone, it helped people to see the form and the overlay at the same time.
The width is set to 60% instead of the standard 80% to make it easier to see the form and to help people on mobile phones not have the overlay cover too much of the screen. The Close button was added to help with accessibility.

Why you should use the overlay instead ...

Over the course of four studies, the Details Summary polyfill was modified to make it look more actionable. 4 of 10 people missed seeing/using the original version. The final version had a box around it, and on hover, the summary looks like a link. This approach was more successful, all 15 of 15 participants saw & used that version. The problem with this component in forms is that it pushes the field down below the expanded details. People in the study had trouble finding the input field once the details were open. The overlay for help solves that problem and other than in mobile, allows people to see both the form itself and the help, so that they connect the concepts. Details summary is also not as easily noticed and used by people using screenreaders.
